# Ontario Budget Diff: 2024-25 Actual vs 2026-27 Plan

*What changed between last year's actual spending and what the government plans to spend next year.*

**Sources:** Ontario 2026 Budget Tables 3.1, 3.5, 3.7, 3.9, 3.10, 3.11 (March 2026)

## Headline

Ontario spent $227.3B in 2024-25 with a $1.1B deficit. For 2026-27, the government plans to spend $244.2B with a $13.8B deficit — the second-largest in Ontario history after the 2020-21 COVID deficit of $16.3B. Spending grows $16.9B (+7.4%) while revenue grows only $5.7B (+2.5%).

### Key Revenue Issues

**PIT growth of 17% in two years is the biggest bet in the budget.** The government is projecting PIT to grow from $55.7B to $65.1B. This requires sustained employment growth, wage increases, and continued equity market gains (capital gains tax revenue). In a tariff environment with potential layoffs and market volatility, this is optimistic. A 1 percentage point drop in nominal GDP growth costs $1,250M in taxation revenue (Table 3.6).

**Other Non-Tax Revenue drop of $8.6B is mostly a one-time effect.** 2024-25 included $6.9B from a court-approved settlement between tobacco companies and their creditors for smoking-related health care costs. This inflates the 2024-25 baseline and makes the revenue "growth" figure misleading. Stripping out the tobacco settlement, underlying revenue growth is closer to $14.3B (+6.9%), not $5.7B.

**GBE Income declining despite LCBO expansion.** Income from Government Business Enterprises drops from $7.5B to $6.5B. The LCBO is seeing lower revenue even as alcohol sales expand to convenience and grocery stores. OLG revenue is also declining. This is partially offset by higher OPG income from nuclear generation.

**Gasoline and Fuel Tax permanently cut.** Both rates permanently reduced (Gasoline by 5.7 cents/litre, Fuel by 5.3 cents/litre). The government claims $2.1B in savings since 2022. Revenue is flat year-over-year, meaning volume growth barely offsets the rate cut. This is approximately $2.3B in annual forgone revenue compared to pre-cut rates.

*Health sector includes Long-Term Care. Table 3.7 shows Health at $91.3B for 2024-25 (actual) when LTC is combined.

### Key Expense Issues

**Health spending growth of $9.9B (+10.8%) dominates the budget.** Health goes from $91.3B to $101.2B, driven by:
- Hospital cost growth (4% annual assumption per Table 3.8)
- OHIP utilization increases (aging population)
- Ontario Public Drug Programs demand growth
- Long-term care staffing mandate (4 hours direct care per resident per day)
- Home care expansion (53 million hours of personal support services)
- $50B hospital construction capital plan (10-year)

**Postsecondary is being cut.** Despite the announced "$6.4B sustainability investment," actual postsecondary spending declines from $14.2B to $14.0B. The $6.4B is spread over multiple years and is offset by lost international student tuition revenue from the federal cap on study permits. The government frames this as increased investment. The numbers show a net cut.

**Debt servicing up 14% — $17.2B buys nothing.** Interest costs grow from $15.1B to $17.2B. By 2028-29, debt servicing reaches $19.7B — more than the entire Justice sector and nearly as much as Children and Social Services. Every dollar of debt servicing is money that does not fund a program, build a road, or treat a patient.

**Justice sector flat despite massive new commitments.** The budget announces new jails (Thunder Bay, Eastern Ontario, Brockville, Niagara, Milton, Sudbury), $32.5M in border security, $1B+ in police training facilities, $41M for school resource officers, and expanded correctional capacity. Yet Justice spending only grows $0.1B. This implies cuts to existing justice programs to fund the new ones.

## Summary: Where the Money Goes

| Category | 2024-25 → 2026-27 | Key Driver |
|----------|-------------------|------------|
| Health | +$9.9B | Hospital costs, drug programs, aging population |
| Debt servicing | +$2.1B | Higher outstanding debt at higher rates |
| Education | +$2.5B | Cost growth, not enrolment growth |
| Other Programs | +$1.8B | Infrastructure, critical minerals |
| Children/Social | +$0.9B | Social assistance demand |
| Justice | +$0.1B | Flat despite new jails and policing |
| Postsecondary | **-$0.2B** | International student cap revenue loss |
| **Total** | **+$16.9B** | — |

**Revenue grows $5.7B. Spending grows $16.9B. The gap is $13.8B in borrowing.**

Of the $16.9B in spending growth, $12.0B is non-discretionary (health cost growth + debt servicing). The government has approximately $4.9B in actual spending choices — and those choices include Highway 413, new jails, border security, and HST rebates, while cutting postsecondary education.
